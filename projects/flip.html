Optimizing family law information gathering and learning for Ontarians through digitizing the in-person program.

Ontario Digital Service Lab

Role

As one of three product designers at Ontario Digital Service Lab working on multiple projects, I wore many hats from project scoping, problem assessment, user recruitment, user research to ideation.

On this particular project, I collaborated closely with two product design interns to assess our problem, recruit vendors for user research, design our prototypes, facilitate 17 user interviews and usability testing sessions, and synthesize and present our findings to our clients over the span of a six week sprint.

Background

The Mandatory Information Program (MIP) is a two-hour, judicially- mandated session that provides family law litigants with information about the family justice system, options available to resolve their family law disputes, and the effects of relationship breakdown on children and adults.

When a litigant files for a court case, they are given a MIP notice that instructs them to attend an in-person MIP session with date and location details. The Family Law Rules require family law litigants to attend a MIP no later than 45 days after a case has been started. Otherwise, their case cannot move forward.

Once a MIP has been attended, the litigant is provided a certificate of completion, which the court files in the continuing record.

The lack of a provincially accepted method for delivering MIPs off-site presents an issue for clients who may not be able to attend in-person, due to injuries, travel restriction or childcare issues.

Problem

We have observed that MIP isn’t meeting the public’s need for early, convenient and multi-channel access to user-friendly information about the family justice system in Ontario, which is causing people to spend too much time and resources navigating the legal system.

How might we improve so that our family law information program is more successful based on increased attendance, and increased uptake of mediation services?

Field Research

I attended a Mandatory Information Program (MIP) session at the Waterloo Region Courthouse to observe the oral and visual delivery of the program, as well as its content design. There were 10 attendees.

Delivery

  • Two presenters read directly from a script in monotone
  • Attendees were not allowed to ask questions throughout the session. They had to line up at the end of the parents section of the program to ask a presenter.
  • The projector screen was on the left wall of the courthouse, which was difficult for people sitting on the left side to see
  • Some attendees were on their phones, dazing off, or emotionally distracted. A few attendees were highlighting the resource package given to them

Content

  • Story-telling, examples and process-related/step-by-step information is missing.
  • Content requires more frequent updating. Some resources were outdated and had broken links.
  • Information needs to be available in multiple languages for Ontarians, whose first language is not English.
  • Slides need to be more visually compelling.

Moving Forward

  1. Content redesign of the program. Content redesign of the program. The content needs to include more examples and interactive activities to accommodate for all styles of learning. The MIP notice and program script needs to be rewritten at a Grade 6 level, as mandated by government standards. I worked on creating example calculations for division of property and simplifying the MIP notice.
  2. Digital channel for the program. As a team, we had sketching sessions together to start the ideation process. With an idea of features we wanted to implement, we started doing research into existing learning management systems.
  3. Integrating the digital alternative with the existing court system . We looked at the entire journey a ligitant would have to go through to receive certificate of completion for the MIP, so we could connect the in-person experience of receiving the notice to the digital alternative.

User Research

We designed recruitment posters for the Ministry of the Attorney General to post in the Family Law Information Centres in courthouses across Ontario.

Government had a timely approval process around hosting surveys. Instead, we directed them to email us, and we responded with the survey questions.

User Interviews

To better understand the overall legal process of filing a court case, where the MIP falls within this process, the pain points that users experience and opportunities for improvement, we conducted user interviews.

Due to the lack of time and proper channels for recruitment, we were only able to recruit 4 people for user interviews. However, amongst the 4 people listed below, we discovered common themes.

  • 2 seperated/divorced individuals
  • 1 individual in the process of separation (taken the in-person MIP)
  • 1 mediator who is a MIP presenter
Key Findings
  • Family law information from government sources are confusing and inaccessible
  • People find family law information through friends and family; they also heavily rely on shared experiences and advice from their lawyers
  • People feel vulnerable throughout this process with added stress from navigating a complex family law system
  • Adding quizzes to the MIP to test comprehension would be stressful for users

Hypothesis 1

Learning Management System

We made an initial hypothesis to use a learning management system. We decided this because it met our high level requirements derived from the existing model.

Existing model
  • In-person sessions have a record of attendance of applicants and respondents
  • Information is divided into different lesson topics
  • Provided resources include a printed slide deck with links to more information
Requirements
  • Have a system that will track users that have taken the program online
  • Optimally organize content and provide multimedia options
    (i.e. videos, embedded hyperlinks)
  • Be able to produce some kind of certification proving completion of the program

Content Redesign

In an effort to show the Ministry of the Attorney General how a slideshow can be enhanced for better learning, we redesigned the slide deck for the division of property lesson in the program to include examples and visuals. We changed the name of the Mandatory Information Program to the Family Law Information Program to be indicative of the contents of the program.

Usability Testing

We conducted usability testing with a total of 7 people to observe how our users interacted with our redesigned notice that directs them to the landing page of the program and the LMS prototype to gather feedback from them.

Our users included a mediator, mental health professional, someone who had taken the MIP and 4 people who have expereienced a separation or divorce, but have not taken the MIP.

LMS Prototype

Positive Findings

  • Privacy policy at a first glance seemed to adhere to Ontario.ca standards (specifically in regards to data collection, storage, and retention)
  • Drag and drop design, so there is a smaller learning curve for the program administrators
  • Customizations can be made to update the program and include necessary features

Negative Findings

  • Out-of-the-box open-source learning management systems have a lot of features that are not applicable to people taking this program
  • Account creation and enrollment process is long and sparks frustration and confusion for users
  • Using an LMS would require a costly Privacy Impact Assessment before implementation
  • Some content is dry and should incorporate some degree of interactiveness to engage people

Moving Forward

The benefits of using off-the-shelf software are reduced, since major customizations most likely need to be made to improve the user experience. After testing two iterations of this hypothesis, we decided that a LMS is not a good solution for offering the MIP because it contains extra features that clog up the user’s experience.

From insights drawn from usability testing, we discovered a new type of user. We created a fictional persona of someone who is in the information gathering stage and have not officially began dispute resolution in or out of court.

Hypothesis 2

Open Access on Ontario.ca

Our next hypothesis was to make the Family Law Information Program available on ontario.ca for people to learn about information applicable to them.

Benefits
  • Users wouldn’t have to create an account to access this information. This reduces the number of barriers between users and the information that they need (i.e. no registration and enrolment process).
  • People would have access to family law information earlier in their journey. This would reduce the chance of mistakenly bringing family law cases to court. It could simultaneously result in an increase in uptake of mediation services.
  • By providing a central hub of information related to separation and divorce, we can reduce the amount of time people spend navigating legal resources.

Positive Findings

  • No account creation means greater accessibility to family law information.
  • Hosting the course on our own domain (ontario.ca) would reduce data and privacy concerns.
  • Iteration of the Family Law Information Program notice is visually appealing and highlights important information effectively

Negative Findings

  • No account creation means we cannot start the course and continue on a different device.
  • Although legal jargon on the Family Law Information Program notice has been heavily reduced, some sentences still spark confusion.

Solution

At the end of the 6 week sprint, we gave a presentation to the executive team at the Ministry of the Attorney General to provide our three-part recommendation as guidance in moving forward with this project.

1

Content

Secure a content strategist/designer to lead a full content design exercise of the curriculum to introduce multi-media into the slide deck and incorporate interactive activities (if possible)

2

Open Access

Offer the digital course openly using search engine indexed pages on Ontario.ca to help people find the information they need and encourage them to complete the program before they get to court.

Retrospect

  1. We could have been more productive had we spent more time understanding the domain of family law. There were times when we had to scrap a prototype because we did not thoroughly understand the legal process.
  2. We put a lot of effort into recruitment with anticipation that participants would be eager to sign up. When designing in a sensitive domain, such as family law, I think reaching out to family, friends and colleagues garners more guarantee for participation. The lengthy email exchange that replaced the initial plan to host a survey online may have also deterred the public from following through with signing up. It is important to doublecheck with the team about mandatory approval processes that may exist.
  3. Content design was a challenge for me on this project. I was unfamiliar with law and had to get up to speed very quickly. With this project, I realized that content design is one of my weak points as a designer. I am working to improve in this aspect as content heavily impacts the effectiveness of user-centred design.
